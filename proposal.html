Section 1

Extended Synopsis

Epigenetic deregulation is a hallmark of cancer, yet how it reshapes genome activity beyond classical gene expression remains poorly understood. Transposable elements (TEs), which constitute ~45% of the human genome and ~30% of the Drosophila genome, are normally kept silent by epigenetic mechanisms including DNA methylation, histone modifications, and Polycomb repression1. However, TE activation is frequently observed in tumors, where it has been linked to genome instability, innate immune signaling, and tumor evolution23.

Despite these observations, fundamental questions remain unanswered: How do oncogenic epigenetic states unlock TE activity? In which tumor cell populations does this occur? And does TE activation contribute to tumor progression or merely accompany it?

TE-TUMOR proposes a paradigm shift: rather than treating TEs as passive byproducts of epigenetic chaos, we hypothesize that TEs act as dynamic sensors and potential drivers of epigenetic instability in cancer. Our preliminary data provide compelling support: loss of Polycomb repression in the Drosophila eye epithelium induces tumors accompanied by robust, clonal activation of the LTR retrotransposon TABOR — upregulated 173-fold (log2FC = +7.43, padj ≈ 0) — in 100% of tumors. Strikingly, TABOR activation correlates with tumor size and is suppressed when the tumor program is blocked, suggesting that TE activation depends on the tumor cell state, not merely on Polycomb loss.

This project will: (1) define how Polycomb repression controls TEs in somatic tissues; (2) determine whether RNA interference cooperates with Polycomb to buffer TE activation; (3) uncover how tumor-specific cell states unlock TE expression and characterize its cellular consequences; and (4) translate findings to human cancer by mining TCGA and single-cell datasets for TE signatures in Polycomb-mutant tumors.

Section 2

Introduction & Scientific Background

2.1 Transposable elements: parasites turned genome architects

Transposable elements are mobile DNA sequences that propagate within genomes through copy-paste (retrotransposons) or cut-paste (DNA transposons) mechanisms. Far from being "junk DNA," TEs have profoundly shaped genome evolution by contributing regulatory sequences, novel genes, and structural variation4. In humans, TEs account for approximately 45% of genomic DNA, with LINE-1, Alu, and endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) being the most abundant families5. In Drosophila melanogaster, TEs represent ~30% of the genome, with LTR retrotransposons (including gypsy, copia, and TABOR families) being particularly prevalent6.

While TEs drive genome evolution over long timescales, their acute mobilization is dangerous: new insertions can disrupt genes, trigger chromosomal rearrangements, and generate double-stranded DNA breaks7. In animal gonads, the piRNA pathway provides a dedicated defense system against TEs8. In somatic tissues, however, TE silencing relies primarily on chromatin-based mechanisms and, in Drosophila, the siRNA pathway9.

2.2 Polycomb group proteins: master epigenetic silencers

Polycomb group (PcG) proteins form two principal complexes — PRC1 and PRC2 — that cooperate to establish and maintain transcriptional silencing10. PRC2, containing the catalytic subunit E(z)/EZH2, tri-methylates histone H3 at lysine 27 (H3K27me3), creating a repressive chromatin mark recognized by PRC111. PRC1, containing Polyhomeotic (Ph) and Posterior sex combs (Psc/BMI1), monoubiquitylates H2AK119 and compacts chromatin12.

PcG proteins are among the most frequently mutated chromatin regulators in human cancer. EZH2 is mutated or overexpressed in lymphoma, melanoma, prostate, bladder, and endometrial cancers13. BMI1 (Psc homolog) is overexpressed in breast, colorectal, lung, and brain cancers14. PHC1/2/3 (Ph homologs) are altered in leukemia and medulloblastoma15. Loss of Polycomb function drives tumorigenesis through deregulation of developmental genes (Hox, Wnt, Notch pathways), but its impact on transposable element expression in somatic tissues has never been systematically investigated.

2.3 The PI's foundational work: E(z) controls chromatin targeting via dual histone marks

The PI recently demonstrated that E(z) — the Drosophila homolog of EZH2 — plays a critical role in directing the heterochromatin protein Rhino to piRNA source loci via a novel dual-mark recognition mechanism (H3K9me3 + H3K27me3)16. This work established that E(z)-dependent H3K27me3 is not merely a silencing mark but a specificity determinant for chromatin protein binding. Importantly, it placed the PI at the unique intersection of Polycomb biology and transposable element regulation — the precise junction that TE-TUMOR now exploits in the cancer context.

2.4 TEs in cancer: emerging evidence and open questions

TE activation in cancer has emerged as a topic of intense interest. In human tumors, LINE-1 retrotransposons can mobilize and cause somatic insertions that contribute to tumor evolution1718. Endogenous retroviruses (ERVs) are reactivated in many cancers and can trigger innate immune responses through production of double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) — a phenomenon termed "viral mimicry" that can sensitize tumors to immune checkpoint blockade1920.

Epigenetic drugs (DNA methyltransferase inhibitors and EZH2 inhibitors) are known to reactivate TEs21, and the EZH2 inhibitor tazemetostat is now FDA-approved for epithelioid sarcoma22. However, whether TE activation contributes to the therapeutic efficacy or represents a dangerous side effect remains unknown.

In Drosophila, the powerful genetic toolkit allows precise dissection of cause and consequence at single-cell resolution within intact tissues — a capability that is impossible to achieve in human tumors. The conservation of core epigenetic machinery (E(z)/EZH2, Ph/PHC, Psc/BMI1, Ago2/AGO2) ensures that mechanistic insights from Drosophila can be directly translated to human cancer biology.

2.5 Drosophila as a model for epigenetic tumorigenesis

The Drosophila eye imaginal disc provides an ideal model for studying Polycomb-driven tumorigenesis. Loss of PRC1 components (Ph, Psc) in the eye disc leads to neoplastic transformation, tumor growth, and metastasis2324. These tumors exhibit hallmarks of human cancer: uncontrolled proliferation, loss of cell polarity, invasion, and activation of conserved oncogenic pathways (JAK-STAT, Zfh1/ZEB)25. The short generation time (10 days), powerful genetic tools (Gal4-UAS, FLP-FRT clonal analysis), and accessibility of intact tissues for imaging make this system uniquely suited for the proposed experiments.

Section 3

Critical Gaps in Knowledge

Despite significant advances in both TE biology and cancer epigenetics, four fundamental questions remain unanswered:

Gap 1 — Polycomb control of TEs in somatic tissues is unexplored

While Polycomb's role in regulating developmental genes is well established, its contribution to TE silencing in somatic (non-gonadal) tissues has never been systematically studied1. The piRNA pathway operates primarily in gonads; how TEs are controlled in somatic tissues where Polycomb is a dominant silencer remains a major open question. Existing TE-cancer studies focus almost exclusively on DNA methylation19, leaving the Polycomb-TE axis entirely unexplored.

Gap 2 — TE activation selectivity is unexplained

When epigenetic silencing is disrupted, not all TEs respond equally — some activate, some are further repressed, and most remain unchanged. Our preliminary RNA-seq data illustrate this strikingly: 6 TEs are upregulated and 14 are downregulated upon Polycomb loss. No current model explains this selectivity. Understanding which defense systems control which TEs — and whether they cooperate or act independently — is critical for predicting TE behavior in cancer.

Gap 3 — The link between tumor cell state and TE activation is unknown

TE activation in cancer is treated as a passive consequence of global epigenetic collapse2. No study has asked whether TE activation depends on the specific tumor cell state rather than on epigenetic loss alone. Our observation that TABOR activation is suppressed when the tumor program is blocked — even though Polycomb remains absent — challenges this view and suggests a fundamentally different model in which TEs are linked to tumor identity.

Gap 4 — Cellular consequences of TE activation in tumors are unmapped

While TE activation has been correlated with genome instability and immune signaling at the population level3, no study has mapped TE activation at single-cell resolution within intact tumors and correlated it with cellular stress markers (DNA damage, dsRNA, apoptosis, proliferation). Bulk approaches average signal across millions of cells, obscuring the critical question: are TE-active cells the ones that are dividing, dying, or both?

TE-TUMOR addresses all four gaps simultaneously. By combining genetic models of Polycomb-driven tumorigenesis with single-cell imaging of TE expression and cellular stress markers, we will determine how Polycomb loss, tumor cell identity, and TE activation are mechanistically connected — and establish whether TEs are passive markers or active contributors to tumor biology.

Section 4

Central Hypothesis

TE-TUMOR Hypothesis

Epigenetic tumor states created by the loss of chromatin repressors such as Polycomb unlock transposable element expression in specific tumor cell populations, where TE activation may contribute to genome instability, cellular stress responses, and tumor heterogeneity.

This hypothesis makes three testable predictions:

  • Prediction 1: Polycomb complexes directly silence a defined subset of TEs in somatic tissues, and this silencing cooperates with the siRNA pathway.
  • Prediction 2: TE activation in tumors depends on the establishment of a specific tumor cell state (defined by JAK-STAT/Zfh1 signaling), not merely on Polycomb loss.
  • Prediction 3: TE-active tumor cells exhibit elevated markers of genome instability (γH2Av), antiviral response (dsRNA/J2), and altered cell fate (proliferation or apoptosis).

Objectives & Specific Aims

Aim 1 — Months 1-30

Define how Polycomb repression controls transposable elements in somatic tissues

Rationale: PcG complexes are major chromatin repressors, but their role in TE regulation in somatic tissues remains poorly understood. Our preliminary data show that loss of Polyhomeotic (PRC1) induces selective TE deregulation (6 up, 14 down), suggesting Polycomb contributes to TE control but cooperates with additional mechanisms.

  • T1.1 Generate PRC1 (Ph, Psc) and PRC2 (E(z), Su(z)12) knockdowns in eye imaginal discs using Ey-Gal4
  • T1.2 Perform strand-specific, ribo-depleted RNA-seq (3 biological replicates per genotype) to identify all deregulated TEs
  • T1.3 Validate top TE candidates by RT-qPCR and single-molecule RNA-FISH
  • T1.4 Perform H3K27me3 and H3K9me3 CUT&RUN in wild-type and mutant eye discs to map chromatin state at TEs
  • T1.5 Bioinformatic integration: correlate TE expression changes with chromatin mark loss/gain
Strand-specific RNA-seq CUT&RUN RT-qPCR RNA-FISH (smFISH) DESeq2 Drosophila genetics
Expected result: A complete map of Polycomb-dependent vs. independent TEs in somatic tissue. Identification of which TE families are directly silenced by H3K27me3 vs. controlled by other mechanisms.
Deliverable: Publication (target: Genes & Development or EMBO Journal).
Aim 2 — Months 9-36

Test whether RNA interference cooperates with Polycomb to buffer TE activation

Rationale: In Drosophila somatic tissues, the endo-siRNA pathway (Ago2-dependent) is a major TE defense mechanism9. We hypothesize that RNAi acts as a compensatory buffer when Polycomb silencing is compromised — explaining why only a subset of TEs escape in Polycomb mutants.

  • T2.1 Profile small RNA populations (siRNAs, miRNAs) in eye discs by small RNA-seq (oxidized + non-oxidized)
  • T2.2 Identify TE-targeting endo-siRNAs and map their genomic origins
  • T2.3 Generate Polycomb + Ago2 double mutant clones (Ph-; Ago2-) using FLP-FRT mosaic analysis
  • T2.4 Compare TE activation profiles: single vs. double mutants (RNA-seq + RNA-FISH)
  • T2.5 Test whether combined loss enhances tumorigenesis and/or genome instability (γH2Av staining)
Small RNA-seq FLP-FRT clonal analysis Ago2 mutants Combinatorial genetics γH2Av immunostaining
Expected result: If RNAi buffers TE activation, double mutants will show synergistic TE derepression far exceeding either single mutant. This would establish the first layered defense model for somatic TE control.
Deliverable: Publication (target: Molecular Cell or EMBO Journal).
Aim 3 — Months 12-48

Determine how tumor cell states unlock TE activation and characterize its cellular consequences

Rationale: Our preliminary data reveal that TABOR activation depends on the tumor state (Zfh1/Stat92E program), not merely on Polycomb loss. This aim will mechanistically dissect this link and map the cellular consequences of TE activation at single-cell resolution within intact tumors.

  • T3.1 Characterize TABOR activation dynamics across tumor developmental stages (early clones → late tumors)
  • T3.2 Test tumor-state dependency: Zfh1 RNAi, Stat92E RNAi, and Stat92E dominant-negative in Polycomb-deficient background
  • T3.3 Combine TE RNA-FISH (TABOR, BLASTOPIA, DM1731) with immunostaining for:

    • Antiviral response: J2 antibody (dsRNA detection)
    • Cell proliferation: PH3 (mitotic index)
    • Apoptosis: Dcp1 (effector caspase)
  • T3.4 Quantitative single-cell image analysis: co-expression scoring, spatial correlation mapping
  • T3.5 Functional tests: TE-directed RNAi (knockdown of TABOR/BLASTOPIA in tumors) to test whether TE silencing modifies tumor behavior
Multiplex RNA-FISH γH2Av / J2 / PH3 / Dcp1 Confocal microscopy Single-cell quantification TE-directed RNAi
Expected result: First single-cell spatial map of TE activation correlated with stress markers in tumors. Determination of whether TE-active cells are preferentially dying, dividing, or stressed.

Section 6

Preliminary Data

We present two key datasets that establish the feasibility and novelty of TE-TUMOR: (i) confocal microscopy showing TABOR RNA activation in Polycomb-deficient tumors, and (ii) genome-wide RNA-seq revealing selective TE deregulation upon Polyhomeotic knockdown.

6.1 TABOR is massively activated in Polycomb-deficient tumors

Using the Ey-Gal4 driver to knock down Psc and Su(z)2 in the eye imaginal disc, we observe robust tumor formation accompanied by striking TABOR activation detected by RNA-FISH. Red signal (TABOR RNA) is specifically enriched in tumor tissue and absent from adjacent wild-type regions.

Key interpretation: The selectivity of TE response (LTR retrotransposons up, telomeric elements HETA/TAHRE/TART down) demonstrates that Polycomb loss does not cause non-specific chromatin decompaction. Section 7

Methodology & Work Packages

7.1 Model system

All experiments use the Drosophila melanogaster eye imaginal disc system. Polycomb mutations are introduced using the Ey-Gal4 driver (eye-specific expression) combined with UAS-RNAi constructs. Clonal analysis uses the FLP-FRT system for mosaic tissue generation. Temporal control is achieved using Gal80ts temperature-sensitive repression.

7.2 Key technologies

  • Transcriptomics: Strand-specific, ribo-depleted RNA-seq with TE-aware quantification pipeline (TEtranscripts/SQuIRE26)
  • Chromatin profiling: CUT&RUN for H3K27me3, H3K9me3, H2AK119ub (low-input, high-resolution27)
  • Small RNA-seq: Oxidized (NaIO4) and non-oxidized libraries to distinguish siRNAs from piRNAs
  • Single-molecule RNA-FISH: Stellaris probes for individual TEs + HCR-FISH for signal amplification
  • Multiplexed imaging: RNA-FISH combined with immunofluorescence (γH2Av, J2, PH3, Dcp1) on same tissue
  • Quantitative image analysis: Custom CellProfiler/QuPath pipelines for single-cell co-expression scoring

Translation to Human Cancer

9.1 Conservation of molecular machinery

The core molecular components investigated in TE-TUMOR are deeply conserved between Drosophila and humans:

Polyhomeotic (Ph)=PHC1/2/3 Altered in leukemia, medulloblastoma, osteosarcoma.

9.2 Planned human data analysis (WP4)

  • Mine TCGA datasets (n > 10,000 tumors) for TE expression patterns in EZH2/BMI1/PHC-mutant vs. wild-type tumors across all cancer types
  • Analyze published single-cell RNA-seq atlases for cell-state-specific TE activation signatures
  • Correlate TE activation with innate immune signatures (interferon response genes, dsRNA sensing pathways)
  • Cross-reference with patient survival data to assess prognostic value of TE signatures
  • Establish clinical collaborations for validation in patient tumor samples (Centre Jean Perrin, Clermont-Ferrand)

9.3 Clinical implications

EZH2 inhibitors and TE activation: Tazemetostat (FDA-approved) and other EZH2 inhibitors are expected to reactivate specific TEs. Our framework predicts which TEs will respond and in which cell types. This could be therapeutically exploited through viral mimicry (TE-driven immune activation sensitizing tumors to checkpoint immunotherapy19), or represent a risk if TE activation drives genome instability. Understanding this is critical for the ~15 ongoing EZH2 inhibitor clinical trials.

Section 11

Risk Management & Contingencies

High impact

TE signals too weak for single-cell FISH in tumors

Mitigation: TABOR shows 173x upregulation — among the strongest possible signals. We use HCR-FISH for amplification. If needed, shift to smFISH with branched DNA amplification. Drosophila tissues are thin and accessible, unlike human biopsies.

Medium impact

Polycomb + Ago2 double mutants lethal

Mitigation: FLP-FRT clonal analysis generates mutant patches in wild-type tissue. Temporal control via Gal80ts. Alternative: partial RNAi knockdown titration series.

Medium impact

TABOR tumor-state dependency not confirmed

Mitigation: M30 go/no-go decision. If TABOR dependency is not confirmed, we pivot to DM1731 or BLASTOPIA (also upregulated). A negative result still yields a publication defining TE-tumor-state independence.

Medium impact

Human TE patterns diverge from fly predictions

Mitigation: Divergence is scientifically interesting — reveals lineage-specific TE control. Fly model provides stand-alone mechanistic value. WP4 is designed as validation, not as the project's foundation.

Low impact

Recruitment delays

Mitigation: PI embedded in GReD (major French genetics center) with strong PhD programs (Ecole Doctorale SVSAE) and international postdoc recruitment networks. Timeline includes 3-month hiring buffer.

Low impact

Sequencing bottlenecks

Mitigation: GReD has on-site access to the GenomAuvergne sequencing platform. Backup: commercial providers (Novogene, BGI) with <4 week turnaround.

12.1 Scientific impact

TE-TUMOR will establish a new conceptual framework

Transposable elements as dynamic sensors and potential drivers of epigenetic instability in cancer. This reframes TEs from passive genome residents to active participants in tumor biology.

By linking Polycomb dysfunction, tumor cell identity, and TE activation at single-cell resolution, this project will provide the first mechanistic understanding of why specific TEs wake up in specific tumor states.
